4000 BC ~ Thames Track

Thames track, which follows the route that is now the A13, is the earliest track through Essex. Archaeological evidence of the oldest settlements of modern humans has been found along this path.

1000 BC ~ The Great East Road

The Great East Road, the second oldest road in Essex, similar to the route of the A12. The Romans paved the road and called it Iter IX. The river crossing was a ford, and the Saxon word for broad was 'rom' - therefore Romford.

900 BC ~ Havering Hoard deposited

Weapons including axe heads, spearheads, fragments of swords, daggers and knives, alongside some other unusual objects rarely found in the UK, make up a total of 453 bronze objects that were probably deposited sometime between 900-800BC and remained undiscovered until 2019.

49 BC ~ Boudicca

Boudicca led her troops to London following the Great East road, almost certainly ravaging the tiny settlement of Durolitum (near Romford) on the way.

946 ~ Pyrgo Palace

Pyrgo Palace (originally Portegore) was probably built as a hunting lodge. Over the following 800 years or so it became the home of a great number of dowager queens and other royal persons. Pyrgo's history is tangled with that of the Royal Palace.

969 ~ Wennington first mentioned

First recorded in 969AD as Winintuna, there is a suggestion that Wennington was named for the Saxon chief, Wynna. It is a small, ancient marshland village bounded on the west and north by Rainham, and east by the village of Aveley, and south by the Thames. Its boundary with Aveley was being disputed in the 19th century but ultimately settled in 1842.

1159 ~ Suttons Manor

Suttons Manor was created by Henry II, who gave all the land between Abbs Cross Lane, Dover's Corner. the River Ingrebourne and Pelt Street (now High Street) to the Monastery of St. Nicolas and St. Bernard of Savoy in Switzerland in thanks for the hospitality given to a royal envoy in difficulties in the St. Bernard Pass between Switzerland and Italy. The name came from an early resident, but Suttons Lane was originally Lake Street, overlooking water now taken up by Hornchurch Railway Station. In 1392Richard II and the Pope agreed this land could be sold to William of Wykeham to endow New College, Oxford, thus creating an absentee landlord for a large part of Hornchurch. The largest residence on the estate was Hackton (not to be confused with Hacton in Upminster) that was roughly where Central Drive is now. Other large properties were Suttons Gate, Suttons Farm, Elm Farm and Sockets (now Albyns Farm, the only one still standing). Suttons Farm can be traced back to 1397, the last farmhouse being Victorian. It was used as an airfield in WW1, reverting to a farm after the war and then becoming RAF Hornchurch in 1928. Suttons Gate stood where Ravenscourt Grove is today and was named after a gate on the north side of the house to stop people having access to Suttons Farm. The gate was removed 1840 and the Georgian property was overtaken by residential development in 1936. A small part of the estate in now the Hornchurch Country Park.


1159 ~ Hornchurch Priory

Founded in 1159, as the hospice of St. Nicholas and St. Bernard, Montjoux (Valais, Switzerland). Hornchurch Priory, which closed in 1391, stood where the (now closed) White Hart was, at the bottom of Hornchurch Hill.

1199 ~ Rainham Ferry

The ferry from Rainham Essex to the Lesnes Abbey, Erith in Kent was the lowest crossing on the Thames until the Gravesend- Tilbury ferry was established in 1268. It probably existed before 1199 but this is the first recorded date for it. The ferry was governed by the tide for operation, downstream on the ebb tide and upstream on the incoming tide for the return journey, it was only steered by a rudder and relied on the tide for power.

1200 ~ Mawneys House

King John granted 'the wood at Romford' to Roger Bigod, the Earl of Norrfolk, at a rent of 5 shillings a year. It stood 150 yards north of Romford High Street on the site now occuppied by the United Services Club. Walter de Mauny held the estate in the 14th centuary when the estate covered 140 acres. It was rebuilt in the 17th century, sold for housing development in 1883 and finally demolished in 1935.


1222 ~ Hornchurch first mentioned

Monasterium Cornutum, which roughly translates as 'church with horn like gables'. Although Roman remains, sufficient to prove a settlement, have been found at Mardyke farm in South Hornchurch, the origins of the main village are obscure. It probably dates from Saxon times, and a church, described in charters and legal documents as the 'Ecclesisa de Havering' once occupied the site of the present Church of St Andrew, which dates from the 13th century. This prominent medieval church was once the principal church in Havering. The unusual Highland bulls head on the eastern gable of the chancel was first mentioned in 1824, but 'points of lead fashioned like horns', apparently in the same position, can be traced back to 1610, and could have even earlier origins. By 1384 a bull's head and horns appeared on the seal of the Prior of Hornchurch, and as well as suggesting that the church had horn-like gables, it could also reflect the area's long association with the leather industry. The 'Hornchurch Hoard,' consisting of 448 silver pennies from around 1223-60 was found by workmen in the Upminster Road, an area within the bounds of the former Hornchurch Priory. They were mostly English coins, struck in the reign of Henry III, with a few contemporary Scottish and Irish coins. At the time of the Domesday survey in 1086, Hornchurch was part of the royal manor of Havering, but from the 13th century onwards it became a flourishing community in its own right. There were at least 10 manors, several farms, and a leather industry that lasted over six centuries. The industry supplied work to a large number of people including fellmongers, tanners and skinners, and the goods produced had an outlet at Romford Market. In 1789 the Old Hornchurch Brewery was founded by John Woodfine, supplying the inns in the area. The economy, involving agriculture and small trades and crafts, developed further in the 18th century when the Wedlake Brothers, Thomas and Robert, opened an innovative iron foundry in Billet Lane. They specialised in agricultural implements, further serving the economy of the area. Thomas lived opposite the foundry at Fairkytes, and like several other gentlefolk who chose to live locally, built or improved houses in the area.


1223 ~ St. Laurence Church

St. Laurence Church has a 13th Century tower, rubble-walled, four square and sturdy buttresses only at the foot. It was much rebuilt by W G Bartlett in 1862-63 but retains the 14th century north arcade with short quatrefoil piers, moulded capitals and double chamfered arches.

1247 ~ Romford Market Charter

Romford Market Charter was granted by Henry III. It was an obvious choice, being half-way between Hornchurch (industry) and Colliers Row (manufacturing charcoal). Under the Charter, no other market could be held within 6 and two-third miles (the distance a flock of sheep could be driven in a day).

1262 ~ Mitchell or Howard's Mill, Hornchurch

The mill was 300 yards from the rear of St. Andrew's Church in the Dell, Hornchurch. A number of mills stood on this site, the last was post mill which closed down in 1912 an was destroyed by fire in 1921

1320 ~ Henry de Yevele

Henry de Yevele, master mason and architect, directed the King's works at Westminster, the Tower of London and Havering Royal Palace.


1323 ~ Romford Old Chapel

was a wooden chapel of ease by the River Rom for the residents of an expanding Romford. It was built after residents had petitioned monks of St. Bernard at St. Andrew's Hornchurch for a place of worship. It survived for 83 years until overcome by rot.

1334 ~ Stubbers first documented

Stubbers has a documented history dating back to 1334, although only the garden wall remains from the original mansion. The estate was named after William Stubbers, a yeoman farmer who had formed it between 1438 and 1463. Humphrey Repton, the great late Georgian landscape designer provided designs for Stubbers in 1796, detailing them in one of the trademark Red Books (so named for their red morocco binding) which he presented to his clients. The Red Book for Stubbers still exists, and is held by the Essex Record Office. William Coys the famous botanist, lived at Stubbers, North Ockendon, until his death in 1627. He cultivated many unusual species in his walled gardens, and established one of the earliest plant collections recorded in England. A hundred years later many of the plants from Stubbers were used to establish Kew Gardens.

1586 ~ Appleton's Almshouses

In 1586 Jane Ayloffe, a widow, bought a house on the south side of High Street for conversion into 3 alms-houses. The alms-houses were given in trust for the aged poor who had lived in Hornchurch for at least 7 years. Appleton's Almshouses were three cottages and gardens in Hornchurch High Street that were let to poor people at 10 shillings 2 pence per annum. They were demolished in 1967.

1588 ~ Queen Elizabeth

Queen Elizabeth who was brought up largely in Havering, is reputed to have stayed at the Royal Palace the night before delivering her famous "I have but the body of a weak and feeble woman..." speech at Tilbury. The Royal Palace was prepared as a command post in case the Spanish Armada had landed on the Thames Estuary.


1592 ~ Francis Quarles

Francis Quarles, born at Stewards, Romford, became a Royalist poet. His most famous work was Emblems (1635), together with Feast of Wormes (1620) and Argalus and Parthenia (1629). Latterly he wrote tracts in defence of Charles I, consequently a great deal of his output was destroyed by Parliamentarians during the Civil Wars. In 1618 he married Ursula Woodgate, they had 18 children. The Quarles family of Romford, divided by the Civil War: Francis Quarles (1592–1644) of Stewards Manor in Romford was poet and Protestant patriot. The Puritans loved his poetry and the Royalists his political pamphlets. His son, Lieutenant Colonel James Quarles, was the most senior officer in Denzil Holles' Parliamentarian regiment at the Battle of Brentford, where he was killed in 1642. Another son, John Quarles (1625–1665), was studying in Oxford when the town became the King's headquarters. He is believed to have served in one of the Royalist companies there in 1642.

1657 ~ William Derham FRS

Worcestershire-born William Derham was appointed Rector of St. Laurence's Church, Upminster in 1689. He lived in the village until 1716, when he was made a Canon of Windsor, although he remained Rector until his death. In addition to his ministerial, Derham served as the local physician and took a keen interest in science and nature. Derham made daily records of Upminster's weather in 1697-99 and 1704-05, researched bird migration, and created a large museum of insects and birds at his rectory. Most famously, he accuratley estimated the speed of sound by having friends fire shotguns at a distance and observing the interval between the the flash and the arrival of the sound. He is buried in the chancel of St. Laurence (although another source says Upminster churchyard), however, by his own request, no memorial was ever erected to him.

1696 ~ General James Oglethorpe (1696-1785)

Founder of the American state of Georgia in 1731. Retired to Cranham Hall. Oglethorpe gained his early experience as a soldier through a commission abroad. He then proved to be a shrewd campaigner on behalf of imprisoned debtors and in 1732 crossed the Atlantic to found the new colony of Georgia. A home for worthy but poor settlers, this would also provide a buffer for South Carolina against Florida, then held by the Spanish. He rejected slavery in his new colony and even brought a group of Cherokee to meet George II in 1735. Back in England again in 1743, he rose through the ranks, fighting against the Jacobites in the rebellion of 1745. He married the heiress of Cranham Hall in 1743 and spent his long retirement there, during which time he became a celebrity in London society

1707 ~ Dagenham Breach

Although there had been flooding from the Thames on to the flood plains of South Hornchurch and Rainham, notably in 1376, 1594 and 1621, the inundation on 29th October created a lasting breach covering 1000 acres. This was partially down to the breaking of a sluice controlled by the marsh-bailiff, Edward Osborne. The Commissioners of the Levels attempted to close it, but all efforts were in vain until (coincidentally) 29th October, 1713. However, on 15th February, 1714 another storm broke through. The Breach was now about 25 feet deep at Low Water Mark and 250 feet wide, so an Act of Parliament was passed on 14th July 1714, charging tolls on all passing vessels to help defray costs. Captain John Perry began work in 1716 and on 4th September 1717 had stopped the Breach, only to have it washed away on 10th the work was finally completed in June 1720, having cost 40,000, but a stretch of water covering 40 acres remained until 1887 when Samuel Williams began to develop the site for Dagenham Dock.

1838 ~ Romford Union Workhouse

Romford Union Workhouse, later Oldchurch Hospital, in Oldchurch Road, was built by Francis Edwards in a long facade of 25 bays with bowed centres. Smaller ranges of workshops behind originally contained the cruciform workhouse (demolished in 1993); a standard design of the period, which allowed the segregation of inmates and their observation from the Master's house in the centre. A separate gate lodge and two-storey block served the casual wards. The chapel built in 1839, was destroyed by fire 1975. The site was cleared in 2007 to make way for housing.

The premises may be seen at any time by application for the key at Mr. Harvey George's Office at Romford, where the Designs and Plans, with the owner's name, are to be delivered, sealed and directed, New Corn Exchange, Romford, for the Committee, on or before the First day of March next. The bank in question was that of Johnson, Johnson and Mann which was bankrupted after it failed in June 1844- it had taken over from another failed Romford bank, Joyner, Surridge and Joyner. The new Corn Exchange was opened for business on Wednesday 5th November 1845. The Essex Herald on 21st October 1845 reported on the new corn exchange and the opportunity to carry out market business in a covered room instead of the open street, as it has hitherto been. It goes on to provide a description, the new building is about 60 ft. by 34 ft.: the side walls are 22 ft in height which gives the room a noble appearance, and the building affords ample light by the roof being nearly all covered in with glass. The stands are arranged on the best principle we have seen; each desk is placed on a temporary platform, raised about 9 inches from the floor and is enclosed (except in front) by a panelled partition, extending a few feet in front of the desk, so that each stand is secluded from the observation of others. A subscription room and other apartments are attached to the exchange, the floor of which being boarded, renders it suitable for every public appropriation. There will also be showrooms opening from the northern end of the exchange for the public exhibition of agricultural implements, and a sale repository for the disposal of property generally. Arthur Cornell described the Corn Exchange as the principal Hall in the town for concerts, public meetings etc . It was used by St Edward's Church for services whilst the new church was built in 1851, the Congregational Chapel in 1883 after fire had damaged the church in South Street and in the First World War it was used by the YMCA and for public meetings of the Romford and District Workers' Association, soon to become the Romford Labour Party, and for trade union meetings. Always in private hands, the Corn Exchange was enlarged in 1861. It was put up for sale in 1924 and finally closed in 1931, though the building stood until partial demolition in 1968 to make way for the new Woolworth's in the High Street, then in 1972, the final part of the building, with the Exchange lettering exposed above the Curtess Shoe Shop in its final days. The Corn Exchange Clock was displayed in Pearson's Jewellers for many years and on the demolition of their premises, the clock was stored in the Central Library in Romford until the Havering Museum was opened in 2010. It was refurbished for the Havering Museum and is now on permanent display there.

1848 ~ Captain Samuel Davis (1848-1926)

When the First World War broke out, Samuel Davis had already been captain of Romford's first official fire brigade for almost 25 years. He was co-founder, having gone into partnership with his fellow builder James Everett Dowsing in 1890, close to their premises in Mawney Road. Even though Romford was a large and growing town, the fire brigade numbered only 13 men, many of whom were initially recruited from Dowsing & Davis' firm. The operated a water tank with a hand pump, on a cart pulled by a pair of horses borrowed from the council dustmen. They had a machine called a quadricycle (essentially two tandem bikes), with the rest of their equipment stored in container between the gears. The brigade attended one particularly notable incident during the First World War. On 14th September 1916, there was a terrific explosion at the Rainham Chemical Works, formally the J.C. and J. Fields factory which manufactured T.N.T. The fire and subsequent explosion was caused by an employee smoking in the room used for drying nitrate soda. Seven people were killed but Davis and his team rescued 69 injured workers. The explosion all but destroyed the factory, blowing much of it into the Thames. For their bravery, each member of Romford Fire Brigade was awarded an O.B.E. on 5th July 1920. Davis' descendants still own his medal and many of them were inspired to follow his example and join the fire service.

1867 ~ Sir Henry Pelly

Born in Upminster, Sir Henry Pelly came from a family of seafarers. In 1914 he was given command of the battle cruiser Tiger. However, he was dogged by problems, namely that the ship was unfinished and that the crew were inexperienced. This perhaps caused a blunder that almost lost the British the battle of Dogger Bank. Acting on intelligence provided by a wireless inter caption, Pelly and his ship were deployed as part of a squadron that engaged a German raiding squadron in the English Channel. Rear Admiral Beatty, the British commander signalled "engage ship in the enemy's line" (ships fought each over in parallel rows). This was misinterpreted by Pelly, who was second in line after Beatty's ship. He helped attack the lead German ship instead, leaving the actual target, the Moltke, free to fire on Beatty, whose ship, The Lion, was badly damaged as a result. Pelly was not the only captain to misinterpret an order that day, but his failure to pursue the German ships when they fled made him a target of an inquiry. He was only saved by the intervention of the First Lord of the Admiralty, Winston Churchill, who did not want to tarnish a battle that had ended in a British victory in spite of key mistakes. Pelly spent the rest of the war in relative ignominy. He was removed from active service in 1916 and appointed commodore of the Royal Navy barracks in Portsmouth where he remained for the rest of the war.

1916 ~ Explosion at Fields Factory

At 2:57pm an explosion ripped through the Fields factory killing 7 men and injuring 80. Formerly a manufacturer of soap and candles, Fields was taken over in 1914 by the War Department and began making a TNT substitute called dinitrophenol. At approximately 2:50pm a fire was discovered in the nitrate of soda drying room. Attempts were made to put it out and several workers started moving the barrels of explosives into the open, but works chemist said "that the dinitrophenol will explode directly". Captain Church, supervising the removal of the barrels, ordered everyone to get away from them and chased the workers off. At 2:57pm an explosion ripped through the Fields factory killing 7 men and injuring 80. It is thought the death toll would have been higher had it not been for Captain Church's actions. The explosion was attended by several local fire brigades including Romford Fire Brigade led by Captain Samuel Davis ( of Dowsing & Davis Builders and Undertakers). They arrived with the factory well ablaze, "their equipment comprising wet cloths to aid breathing, a whistle and a hank of rope to keep them together, enabled them to remove twelve young women who all survived. Minutes later the factory was blown hundreds of feet into the River Thames". Altogether they rescued 69 people, for which six of the fire crew were awarded OBEs by the King.

1920 ~ Peter Blundell

Peter Blundell was a tramp who at one time lived in a sand bin on the A127 Southend Arterial Road underneath the Hall Lane flyover where he often displayed his paintings of ships. He was a very familiar sight in Romford and is renowned for his paintings of ships on pieces of cardboard which he displayed outside the Central Library in St Edward's Way, Romford. An article in the Romford Recorder of 18th April 1975 includes a photograph of him clean shaven during a stay in Harold Wood Hospital. At the time of the article he was 55 years old and he had been in the hospital for six weeks. Doctors were appealing for a home for him and were reluctant to discharge him from hospital until he had somewhere to go. Mr Blundell was reported as saying "All I want to do is live for a long time. If that means retiring from the road then that's what I will have to do. I don't want to be a nuisance but I want to do what the doctor advises". He was born on January 10th 1920 in Norwich and was the second oldest in a family of four, with three sisters Mary, Margaret and Ann. He lived with his family in Billericay and Romford before joining the Merchant Navy - his father, grandfather and great-grandfather all served in the Royal Navy.; Henry, his father, retired as a lieutenant commander and was awarded an MBE. Following a marriage which broke up he took to the road. On January 5th 1987 he was admitted to Harold Wood Hospital on the advice of London Borough of Havering Social Services staff after complaining of feeling unwell. He had twice refused medical help from social services and the police. Peter Blundell died on Wednesday January 23rd 1987 in Harold Wood Hospital aged 67.  His sister Margaret revealed that his family had often tried to get him to live with them but that he chose to live rough. She organised his funeral which was attended by family and people who knew him from Havering. His cremation took place at Chelmsford on 27th January and his ashes were buried in Stock churchyard.

1943 ~ Raimund Sanders Draper (1913-43)

An American volunteer in the RAF lost his life when the engine in his Spitfire failed on take-off from RAF Hornchurch. Born 27th December 1913 in London, he moved to Manhattan, New York, USA. Although still living in New York, he joined the RAF as a volunteer pilot in 1940. On taking off from RAF Hornchurch on 24th March 1943, he experienced engine problems with Suttons Junior and Senior Schools in the direct path of his Spitfire. Rather than bail out and risk his plane crashing into the school, he crash-landed on the school field, which the plane skidded across, coming to rest against the wall of one classroom. Despite the school being full of pupils, only one 13-year-old was injured by flying glass from one window.

1944 ~ Joseph Holt

He later emigrated to Canada, passing away in 2012.

1990 ~ Havering Street Hockey Club

The history of the club starts in approximately 1987/88 when the Dagenham-based DYNAMIC DAGGERS was formed. The team, which included players from all over Havering, had some success in their matches but not in their choice of leader. He had his own agenda, which included lining his pockets with the club money, leaving the players and their parents out of pocket and unable to register the team in the league.

At this point the remaining players approached Dave Jordan for help in reforming their team. Dave not only assisted the players with sponsorship but called together the parents and formed the BARBARIANS STREET HOCKEY CLUB, with a fully constituted committee. The Barbarians club consisted of one junior team, one peewee team and one senior team. Shortly after, there were so many peewee players that a second team was formed and so the LIBERATORS were born.

The teams had a fairly successful 1988/89 and 1989/90 season. The 1990 season presented the Barbarian club with a problem: the growing number of teams within the club were all using different names. It was decided that the club should adopt a neutral name, and so after much debate the HAVERING STREET HOCKEY CLUB was formed.

2000 ~ RSPB Rainham Marshes

Starting in 2000 the RSPB acquired 411 hectares of medieval marsh land in the Rainham area from the Ministry of Defence that had been used as a firing range for the last 100 years by the British Army. RSPB Rainham Marshes environmentally friendly visitor centre opened to the public in 2006 at a cost of £2,000,000. Rainham Marshes protects 411 hectares of ancient, low-lying grazing marsh in the Thames Estuary. Its complex of wet grassland and ditches, together with grassland and scrub, supports many breeding and wintering birds. Wildlife also includes scarce wetland plants and insects, and a key population of the nationally declining water vole.
