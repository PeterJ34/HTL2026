A short history of .....

Cranham is an ancient parish bounded by Upminster on the west, and North Ockenden on the south. In the Middle Ages Cranham was more often known as Bishop's Ockenden since its chief manor was held by the bishop of London, but from the 15th century it was usually called Cranham. Domesday figures show Cranham as small and heavily wooded, but from the 15th century the process of forest clearance was advancing. Mixed farming, with arable predominating, became the economic mainstay of the parish throughout this time and into the 19th century. The population grew in the 19th century and there were 340 people living in farms or cottages in 1851. After the Second World War Cranham became a commuter suburb of London, with livery stables and riding schools largely replacing sheep and dairy farming. North Ockendon is a small country parish whose medieval name, 'Ockenden Setfountayns', derived from the lords of the manor, the Setfountayn family who acquired the manor in the 1200s. Settlement in the area was established in the Middle Ages, with a central village and a few outlying farms. Little has changed in the makeup of the parish over time, with farming remaining the main occupation. A mill once stood in North Ockendon, but was demolished in 1840. Among the many surviving buildings of historic interest are The Old Bakehouse, The Forge, both timber-framed buildings that were built on the Ockendon Road in the 17th century, and a rectory house that was rebuilt in 1750. North Ockendon Hall was destroyed during WWII, but its medieval moat is now part of Hall Farm flower nursery. In the 19th century some agricultural land was turned over to market gardening, and then some to gravel extraction by the middle of the 20th century. The village became part of Hornchurch Urban District in the 1930s, and then the London Borough of Havering in 1965.

Some People and Places in the Cranham & North Ockendon Story

General James Oglethorpe was a politician, social reformer and military leader. In 1722 he successfully ran for Parliament, and in 1729 he campaigned for prison reform in England. He had a long and varied career, which included founding the North American colony of Georgia in 1731. After many years abroad he met heiress Elizabeth Wright on a return visit to England in 1744. They married and lived at her inherited estate, Cranham Hall, until his death in 1785. Cranham Hall, The Chase, Cranham. Whilst it is known that there was a house on the site of Cranham Hall in about 1600, it was demolished in about 1789, and the present house on the site dates from around 1800. The nearby All Saints Church, was built in 1873, but an original church on the site was recorded in 1310. There is a commemorative tablet to General Oglethorpe, one of the Hall's most notable inhabitants, on its south wall. The Church of St Mary Magdalene in Church Lane, North Ockendon, was built prior to 1075 on a site dating back to c630, but after many alterations through the centuries, only the south doorway of the church now dates from this time. The chancel dates from the 14th century, and the pulpit from the 17th century. There was further restoration and improvements made in the 19th century. Stubbers has a documented history dating back to 1334, although only the garden wall remains from the original mansion. The estate was named after William Stubbers, a yeoman farmer who had formed it between 1438 and 1463. Humphrey Repton, the great late Georgian landscape designer provided designs for Stubbers in 1796, detailing them in one of the trademark Red Books (so named for their red morocco binding) which he presented to his clients. The Red Book for Stubbers still exists, and is held by the Essex Record Office.

William Coys the famous botanist, lived at Stubbers, North Ockendon, until his death in 1627. He cultivated many unusual species in his walled gardens, and established one of the earliest plant collections recorded in England. A hundred years later many of the plants from Stubbers were used to establish Kew Gardens.

Evidence of a Romano-British farmstead, said to have been occupied during the 1st and 3rd centuries, was discovered west of Corbets Tey during gravel digging in Upminster in 1962. In medieval Upminster, settlement seems to have been in three clusters: the village itself, and the hamlets of Hacton and Corbets Tey, which expanded slowly, with a few scattered farms being built at the north and south ends of the parish. Although it was completely rebuilt in 1861-62, St Laurence's church on the Corbets Tey Road retains a tower with stonework dating to around 1200, and it is believed a church may have existed on or near this site as early as the 7th century. There were three manors recorded in Upminster in 1086, the largest of which, originally known as the manor of Upminster, was later known as Gaynes, or Engaynes, after Vitalis Engayne who acquired it in 1214. New Place began as part of the Gaynes estate in 1556, but in 1775 a new house was built on the site by Sir James Esdaile, who developed several of the houses in Upminster. He added a clock turret, which came from Woolwich Arsenal, to the roof of the stable block. It houses a clock made in 1774 by famous London clock maker, Edward Tutet. New Place itself was demolished in 1924, but the stable block remained and is known today as the Clockhouse. The third manor of Upminster, also known as Waltham Upminster and Upminster Hall, was gifted to the college (later abbey) of Waltham Holy Cross in the 9th century by Earl Harold. It is said that today the ghosts of the monks can be glimpsed in a tree walk in the nearby playing fields that were part of the estate! The manor saw many changes in ownership over the years until 1686 when it was bought by Andrew Branfil, and it remained in his family until the early 20th century. Today it is home to the Upminster Golf Club. The economic history of Upminster has chiefly been that of mixed agriculture, with an upsurge in market-gardening in the 19th century. This agricultural heritage is evidenced in two fine monuments still to be seen today. As well as Upminster Windmill of 1803, there is Upminster Barn (also known as Upminster 'Tithe' Barn), which is thought to have been built in the early 15th century, and was used as a threshing barn. Both are now heritage attractions open to the public.

Some People and Places in the Upminster Story

Upminster Windmill stands on Upminster Hill and was built by local farmer James Nokes in 1803. It is the largest smock mill remaining in the London area, and considered to be one of the finest examples. It is now open to the public as a heritage attraction. Alice Perrers lived at Gaynes Manor, Upminster. She died there in 1400 after a life of great notoriety. She was married to Sir William of Windsor, Viceroy of Ireland, but was also the notorious mistress of Edward III. Her influence over the king led to accusations of witchcraft in 1376. William Derham was vicar of Upminster from 1689 to 1735. A natural philosopher, and the author of many research papers, he is credited with producing the earliest, reasonably accurate estimate of the speed of sound. He became a fellow of the Royal Society in 1702, and lived in High House until his death in 1735. Lady Edna Clarke Hall, an artist and poet, lived in Great House, a 16th century farmhouse in Upminster. She studied at the Slade School of Art in 1895-9 and had several exhibitions from 1901 onwards. Several of her drawings are held in the Tate Gallery. She died in 1979, aged 100. Henria Williams lived in Upminster and was active in the Suffragette movement. In a march on the House of Commons in 1910 there was a disturbance that would become known as 'Black Friday'. Many protesters, including Williams, were badly injured by police. Sadly, Williams died suddenly two months later from heart failure. Ian Dury, painter, musician and actor was born in Harrow in 1942, but after his parents separated he moved with his mother to Cranham. He reflected his teenage Teddy boy life around Upminster in the song 'Upminster Kids,' and titled one of his albums 'Lord Upminster'.

First recorded in 969AD as 'Winintuna', there is a suggestion that Wennington was named for the Saxon chief, Wynna. It is a small, ancient marshland village bounded on the west and north by Rainham, and east by the village of Aveley, and south by the Thames. Its boundary with Aveley was being disputed in the 19th century but ultimately settled in 1842. A manor house existed in 1198, known as Wennington Westminster or Wennington Hall, and the church of St Mary and St Peter was also built about this time. The church stands on a ridge of land across the marshes, and is the only remaining medieval building in the village, although it has seen many changes and alterations. The 12th century doorway remains but most of the present church dates from the 13th century, and the tower from the 15th century. It is believed there had also been a Saxon church on this site, and a church is officially recorded in Wennington in 1042-1044 when Edward the Confessor confirmed the parish as belonging to Westminster Abbey. In 1066, the recorded population of Wennington had been eight, but by 1670 there were twelve houses, and by 1801 there were 91 recorded inhabitants. In the 1880s, when wealthy Rainham industrialists chose to live in Wennington, some prosperity came to the area and the population began to rise, but Wennington remained a rural parish, with agriculture as its main occupation and pasture land predominant. Market gardening had become well established by about 1841, and Wennington was producing large quantities of vegetables, especially peas for the London market. From 1865 onwards, several factories and manufacturers tried to set up in Wennington but with less success. Firstly, the Fresh Meat Preserving Company was founded, but passed to James Ingram & Sons, india-rubber manufacturers, just one year later. In 1881 chemical manufacturers, John Kidd & Company, took over, and they also built a coffee house c1882, which survived until 1973. They leased the factory in 1885 to the Camden Marine Steam laundry, but it burnt down in 1891. Wennington was part of Hornchurch Urban district from 1934, until it became part of Havering in 1965. 2019 the Havering Hoard was discovered in Wennington during an archaeological survey prior to a new gravel pit being opened up. The 453 pieces of bronze was the largest hoard discovered in London and the third .

Some People and Places in the Wennington Story

Henry of Yevele was a medieval master-mason and architect who held lands in Wennington. He was involved in work on the Bloody Tower in the Tower of London, and the naves of Westminster Abbey and Canterbury Cathedral Wennington Hall has a history dating back to the Domesday Book. It was held by Edward the Confessor in the 11th century, and in the 14th century by Henry of Yevele, who served as Speaker in two Parliaments of Richard II. However, Wennington Hall has been rebuilt and undergone many alterations, and is today a modern 20th century building. Nicholas Brady was rector in Wennington in the late 19th century, and lived at Rainham Hall. He compiled careful notes on the history of the parish, and took the lead in the social life of Wennington, encouraging concerts, magic lantern shows and glee clubs, mostly centred at the newly built school. Wennington School was built in 1887 adjoining the church. Until then, children had been taught in private houses, and in a thatched cottage in Church Lane that had been owned by the churchwardens. The cottage has since been demolished, and the school was converted into three dwellings in 1966. Henry Perigal, a bookkeeper and eccentric amateur mathematician, was a descendent of a Huguenot family which had immigrated to England in the 17th century. His life spanned almost the entire 19th century. Although he wrote on many subjects, he is primarily known for providing proof for the Pythagorean Theorem in his booklet, Geometric Dissections and Transpositions, the details of which are recorded on his memorial in the churchyard of St Mary & St Peter. He was a member of the London Mathematical Society, treasurer of the Royal Meteorological Society, a fellow of the Royal Astronomical Society, and, at the age of 94, he became a member of the Royal Institution. Did you know? A reformatory ship, called the Cornwall, was moored at nearby Purfleet in 1859? It could accommodate up to 250 boys who had been committed to detention for more than three years. Some terraced houses for its staff were built in Wennington, as well as a large laundry. We have a reminder that the laundry once stood here in the name of Laundry Cottages on Wennington Road.

Early signs of human occupation in Romford include a possible British camp in the Marshalls area and bronzesmith's hoard. Later the area was the site of a Roman staging post called Durolitum. Romford's economy relied on the intersection of major transport routes from an early date. The Middle Ages saw the growth of this small hamlet into a market town. The leather industry which originated in Hornchurch found a ready market in the number of travellers using the old Roman road to Colchester. In 1247, the fledgling market received a royal grant from Henry III, and continues to this day. The existence of the market created a cluster of buildings over the centuries, many of them inns catering for the travellers along the Great Essex Road, and the many visitors to the market. A further boost was given to Romford when improved toll roads were introduced, heralding the rise of the coaching age, where stops were made along the way. Romford now had a very large number of inns and other facilities to cater for the coaches; it even had fashionable shops catering to the gentry and the new professional classes who were setting up in Romford. It was also on the route of itinerants who travelled around the country making a scant living by selling various wares or performing - the type of character seen in Victorian journalist Henry Mayhew's books about the poor. A bombshell hit Romford in the 1830s, with the arrival of the railway. From being a parochial Essex town, slightly cut off, except on market days, Romford now became firmly joined to the rest of Britain and London via the Eastern Counties Railway. One of the main businesses to benefit from the railway was Romford Brewery, which had been founded in 1799. The Brewery closed in 1997, and all that remains is The Brewery Gate, the ground floor being converted into Havering Museum which opened in 2010. Today the Museum can reveal how much more there is in the stories of Romford, Hornchurch, Upminster, Rainham, Havering-atte-Bower and the surrounding areas.

Some People and Places in the Romford Story

The Market began as a sheep market in 1247, with King Henry III granting Romford permission for a weekly market, on a Wednesday, with the addition of a yearly fair from 1250. The Golden Lion Pub that stands at the edge of the Market Place existed in 1440, and still functions today. A largely timber-framed building, it is made up of at least three sections of varying dates, with additions and changes made from the 16th to the 19th centuries. The infamous highwayman Dick Turpin was said to have been a visitor. Church House in the Market Place began as a much larger 15th century building and was used as a chantry priest's house. It was later used as the Cock and Bell Inn, and is rumoured to have a ghost. Sir Francis Bacon, the Elizabethan statesman, philosopher, essayist and author, became landlord of the Golden Lion pub when he inherited it from his father in 1601. Francis Quarles, the Cavalier poet, was born in Romford in 1582. In 1639 he was made city chronologer, a post previously held by Ben Johnson and Thomas Middleton. At the outbreak of the Civil War in 1642, he took the Royalist side and published pamphlets in support of the King. Colonel Thomas Blood (c1618-1680) was born in County Meath, Ireland. A rebel and a republican, his audacious adventures involved him dressing in a variety of disguises. A turncoat and a spy, he is most notorious for attempting to steal the Crown Jewels in 1671, disguised as a parson. Blood and his cohorts were imprisoned briefly in the Tower of London, but curiously he was never actually punished for the crime. Instead, he was rewarded by Charles II with lands in Ireland and a Â£500 annual pension!

Rainham is one of the most significant historic villages in the Havering area. It has roots going back to the Saxon period, and there is important evidence of a settlement from long before that, with a Neolithic site being excavated west of Launders Lane in 1963. There are also remains of a submerged Neolithic forest, which was discovered close to the mouth of the Mardyke River in Rainham marshes. An Anglo-Saxon burial ground from the 6th and 7th centuries was discovered in excavations at Gerpins Pit in 1930. A wealth of items such as swords, brooches, rings, spear heads, and a pair of drinking horns were unearthed, as well as indications of even earlier burials. Evidence of a Roman presence has also been found, with Romano-British pottery shards uncovered at Rainham Ferry, and many Roman artefacts found at the Mardyke Farm Estate in 1963. During the Middle Ages the settlement of Rainham was grouped around the church of St Helen and St Giles and several manor-houses, including Rainham, Berwick, and Gerpins. By the 17th century Rainham Ferry and Rainham Wharf were well established, with goods and passengers coming from London, and the travellers being well provided for by several inns in the area. The 18th century saw increased trade, resulting in several new buildings in the area, most notably Rainham Hall. Rainham had always drawn most of its living from farming, and from the River Thames, and continued to do so into the 20th century, and by 1929 most large farms in the area were known as market gardens. However, the 19th century had brought increased industrialisation in the form of chemical and fertilizer factories at Rainham Ferry. Murex Limited, iron-founders and ferroalloy manufacturers, moved to the area in 1917.

Some People and Places in the Rainham Story

St Helen & St Giles Church is the oldest building in Havering, built c1170, and of much architectural interest. It still has many of its original features, and there is a rare graffito of a medieval ship at anchor on the wall of a stairwell. Richard de Lucy was Chief Judiciary to King Stephen. He was excommunicated by Archbishop of Canterbury Thomas Becket in 1166 and again in 1169. Becket's murder, by the king's henchmen, in 1170 resulted in part from his refusal to lift such sentences of excommunication, and it is said that de Lucy founded St Helens & St Giles church in penance for the death. Berwick Manor in Berwick Pond Road, dates from the 17th century. There have been many alterations and additions since. In 1960 it became an old people's home, and in 1970 it became Berwick Manor country club. Captain John Harle, a wealthy London merchant and master mariner who settled in Rainham. He renovated Rainham Wharf, increasing trade with London and abroad. Importing building materials such as timber from Scandinavia, as well as coal, he also advertised granaries for the storage of corn, and shipments to London twice a week. He prospered and had Rainham Hall built in 1729. Rainham War Memorial Clock Tower The memorial is a short hexagonal clock tower with faces on three sides. It was built in 1920 and stands in the centre of the village at Broadway. It now commemorates soldiers killed in both World Wars, as well as civilian casualties of World War II.

Rainham Hall retains some of its original features, including Delft tiles in the fireplace, and trompe l'oeil frescoes on the walls? A Victorian doghouse can still be seen, with space for three dogs, 'very necessary defences for the owners' coaches against highwaymen'!

The name 'Hornchurch' was first recorded in 1222. It is an Anglicised version of the Latin, 'Monasterium Cornutum', which roughly translates as 'church with horn like gables'. Although Roman remains, sufficient to prove a settlement, have been found at Mardyke farm in South Hornchurch, the origins of the main village are obscure. It probably dates from Saxon times, and a church, described in charters and legal documents as the 'Ecclesisa de Havering' once occupied the site of the present Church of St Andrew, which dates from the 13th century. This prominent medieval church was once the principal church in Havering. The unusual Highland bulls head on the eastern gable of the chancel was first mentioned in 1824, but 'points of lead fashioned like horns', apparently in the same position, can be traced back to 1610, and could have even earlier origins. By 1384 a bull's head and horns appeared on the seal of the Prior of Hornchurch, and as well as suggesting that the church had horn-like gables, it could also reflect the area's long association with the leather industry. The 'Hornchurch Hoard,' consisting of 448 silver of pennies from around 1223-60 was found by workmen in the Upminster Road, an area within the bounds of the former Hornchurch Priory. They were mostly English coins, struck in the reign of Henry III, with a few contemporary Scottish and Irish coins. At the time of the Domesday survey in 1086, Hornchurch was part of the royal manor of Havering, but from the 13th century onwards it became a flourishing community in its own right. There were at least 10 manors, several farms, and a leather industry that lasted over six centuries. The industry supplied work to a large number of people including fellmongers, tanners and skinners, and the goods produced had an outlet at Romford Market. In 1789 the Old Hornchurch Brewery was founded by John Woodfine, supplying the inns in the area. The economy, involving agriculture and small trades and crafts, developed further in the 18th century when the Wedlake Brothers, Thomas and Robert, opened an innovative iron foundry in Billet Lane. They specialised in agricultural implements, further serving the economy of the area. Thomas lived opposite the foundry at Fairkytes, and like several other gentlefolk who chose to live locally, built or improved houses in the area.

Some people and places in the Hornchurch story

Appleton's alms-houses. In 1586 Jane Ayloffe, a widow, bought a house on the south side of High Street for conversion into 3 alms-houses. The alms-houses were given in trust for the aged poor who had lived in Hornchurch for at least 7 years. Thomas Witherings lived with his wife Dorothy at the large manor house of Great Nelmes. When King Charles opened up the post to the public in 1635 it is believed that Witherings, the Postmaster General, produced the plans for the service, one of the most important developments in the history of Britain's postal service. Alice Aylett made provision in her will of 1731 for the founding of Aylett's school, with funding for a schoolmaster to give tuition to ten poor boys. Prior to this, education in Hornchurch was provided by the local clergy, or by charity schools. Bretons manor house was probably named for the LeBreton family who owned the land here from the 12th to the 14th century. Rebuilt in around 1740, it has many of its original features intact. The staircase survives from an earlier Tudor house, and nearby is a brick barn and walled garden that date from the 16th century. There are tales of tunnels, probably of Tudor origin and large enough to take a horse and cart, running from under the house to the river. The purpose of the tunnels is unknown, but they could suggest smuggling activity! Fairkytes house was built in the late 17th century, with additional work being carried out in the 18th and 19th centuries. The first occupant was Job Alibone, whose son Sir Richard Alibone, was the first Catholic to be a Justice of the King's Bench. The Council used Fairkytes as a library from 1953 and since 1972 it has been the home of Havering Arts Centre. Joseph Fry, son of Quaker and prison reformer Elizabeth Fry, lived at Fairkytes house with his wife and children from 1870 until his death in 1896. He, like his mother, engaged in charitable works.

Evidence of a Roman settlement, indicative of agricultural and industrial activity in Havering, was unearthed here in the 1970s. Pottery and much building rubble was discovered along with a group cremation burial site, as well as gullies and ditches associated with metal-working. Havering-atte-Bower was one of the three former parishes that from 1465 to 1892 formed the Royal Liberty of Havering, the others being Hornchurch and Romford. In 1272 it is recorded as 'Hauering atte Bower', with the suffix meaning 'at the royal residence', and this sparsely populated rural village is steeped in royal history. The first royal palace There may have been a palace in Havering-atte-Bower as early as the seventh century, during Saxon times. It was possibly established by Sigeberht the Little (King of Essex 617-653), who was active in this area. It would have been a barn-like building constructed of wood; no indication of its location remains. The second royal palace This palace was likely to have been built by Edward the Confessor (King of England 1042-66) in the middle of the 11th century. Edward the Confessor gave the manor of Havering as a gift to Harold Godwinson, who later succeeded him as King Harold II. When Harold was killed at the Battle of Hastings, the manor passed to the new king, William the Conqueror (r. 1066-87). The third royal palace Henry III (r. 1216-72) greatly remodelled Havering Palace in Kentish rag-stone during the 1250s. He then granted the manor of Havering to his wife, Eleanor of Provence, in 1267. Many monarchs and their wives stayed at Havering and several of them made significant alterations to the Palace. Plans of the ground and first floors were drawn up in 1578 by Lord Burghley, Queen Elizabeth I's chief minister. Pyrgo Palace The origins of Pyrgo Palace are unclear. It is commonly thought to have been the dower palace given to the English queens, but there is no written or archaeological evidence to suggest it existed earlier than the late 15th century. It briefly became a royal palace when Henry VIII bought it from Sir Brian Tuke in the mid-1540s, but Elizabeth I gave it away to Sir John Grey in 1559. He was the uncle of Lady Jane Grey, the 'Nine Days Queen'.

Some people and places in the Havering-atte-Bower story

Isabella of Valois was only 7 years old when she married Richard II in 1396, but she was widowed three years later when her husband was dethroned and murdered. She was held at Havering Palace until she was repatriated to France in 1403. Joanna of Navarre, the second wife of Henry IV (r. 1399-1413), was accused of witchcraft, but later pardoned. She died in some disgrace at Havering in 1437, and her ghost is said to haunt the site of the Palace. Edward VI spent most of his childhood at Havering Palace and was often visited by his sisters, the princesses Elizabeth and Mary, and their cousin Lady Jane Grey. Edward became king at the age of 9, and Sir Anthony Cooke of Gidea Hall, notable for his interest in the education of his five daughters as well as his sons, was said to have been a tutor to young Edward. There is no official record of this, and he is more likely to have been a companion and an intellectual guide. Elizabeth I was a frequent visitor to Havering, and is reputed to have stayed in Havering Palace the night before she delivered the 'I have the body of a weak and feeble woman, but I have the heart and stomach of a king' speech at Tilbury in 1588. Dame Anne Tipping, left a bequest for the education of local children, and the village school was founded in 1724, and named for her in honour of the bequest. It was rebuilt in 1837, and the present building dates from 1891. Bower House Orange Tree Hill, is a mansion built in 1729 for John Baynes, Serjeant-at-Law, (a high ranking barrister), using stone quarried from the ruins of Havering Palace, including an angel bust corbel stone bearing the arms of Edward III. It is now a Christian training centre. The Round House on Broxhill Road is a building in Havering of special note. Built in the 1790s for wealthy tea merchant William Sheldon, it is said to have been designed in the shape of a tea caddy to commemorate how Sheldon had made his fortune.


Did you know?

Elizabeth Balls, born c1761, was one of Havering's most eccentric residents. Also known as 'The Goat Woman of Havering-atte-Bower', she shared her home with a herd of goats, a flock of sheep, a few cats, and a dog. No visit to Havering was said to be complete without dropping by to see her, and the animals whom she called 'her dear children'.
